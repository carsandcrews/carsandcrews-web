**@Jared** (reply #4):
Don't go into the purchase with an emotional attachment before you've seen the car.  Have a price in mind and stick to it. Keep in mind that project cars typically don't return a profit once sold unless they are bought at the right price (cheap) and you are able to do everything yourself.  Even then the chances of being up-side-down on the project are likely.  I'm at least $10K in the hole on mine if I were to sell it today and mine didn't need body work and paint when I got it.  Buying the best car you can within your budget is almost always the most cost effective approach to the car hobby.

Side note:  I'm also looking for another project.  Unlike you, I'm looking at doing a late model build with the end goal being a track capable car for open road course events.  My kid has been doing them with a Miata and it looks like a bunch of fun.  I looked at an NA Pontiac Solstice last weekend.  It fit exactly what I was looking for in that I wouldn't be ruining a future classic by installing a roll bar and doing other track prep work.  The car was priced really cheap because it needed a water pump (runs off the timing chain on those).  I said from the beginning that I wouldn't get into a bidding war over it.  It ended up selling for less than $200 over full asking.  My decision to not bid higher on it holds and I have no regrets.  Another car will pop up.

**@Sick467** (reply #7):
I'm trying to think of a classic that can make money and falling short of ideas.  Short of finding that great deal on the initial purchase, a money maker can be difficult to acquire.  And to be honest, once you start spending money on the project...you risk losing money.  The best money makers are ones that are buy cheap and sell before they come off the trailer.  Meaning you buy and sell it without working on it.  This holds true especially if you're not doing the work yourself.  The folks that are making money on flipping cars are finding a good driver to begin with and putting lipstick on it, then selling to those who get excited about the looks and not necessarily the overall condition.

Outside of the above, you want to pick a car/truck that has plenty of aftermarket support and those are typically your Cheby trucks and Camaros or Rustangs.  The popular Pontiacs and Mopars have good aftermarket support, but their original production numbers are much lower than the other popular classics causing the repop parts to be much more expensive.  Stay away from the rarer classics unless you like mining for original parts that are not reproduced and paying dearly for them.

I'm not trying to squash the idea, but I know I can't make money on a project since I can't stop myself from digging deeper, working more, and spending more.  I do it for the journey and want the newest old car I can build at the end.

I think the key is having patience with the initial search for "the car" that is being sold for less than market value...think "little old lady" sales.

@BLK69JUDGE makes an excellent point regarding sticking with what you know.  If were to build another 67, I would save countless hours of research compared to building a 69, or worse a Mopar.

Stay away from dealers, pick out the years of cars that will work for you, and look for that car in survivor type condition, meaning little to no rust and possibly in running condition.
